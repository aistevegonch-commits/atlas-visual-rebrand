Section 01

Pre-Production

This is where projects are won or lost before you ever hit record. Most people rush this. Do not be most people.

⚠ Brutal Truth
The #1 reason shoots go bad: you didn't scout the location. You assumed the lighting, assumed the access, assumed the client knew what they wanted. Every assumption you make in pre-production becomes a crisis on set.

Client Intake — Questions That Matter

Ask every single one of these on the discovery call. Do not skip questions because the client seems busy. Their impatience in pre-production becomes your emergency in post.

Discovery Call Checklist
  • What is the primary purpose of this video? (Investment pitch, leasing, brand awareness, event recap)
  • Who is the audience? (Investors, tenants, brokers, internal stakeholders)
  • Where will this live? (Website hero, LinkedIn, email campaign, conference loop, TV)
  • What is the desired runtime? (60 sec social, 2-3 min tour, 5+ min documentary)
  • What is the hard deadline and why? (Board meeting, listing launch, conference date)
  • Who are the decision makers? (Will someone else need to approve beyond our primary contact?)
  • Are there brand guidelines, fonts, or color requirements?
  • Are there previous videos they loved or hated? Get links.
  • Is there talent / on-camera talent needed? (Executives, actors, voiceover)
  • Are there restricted areas, active construction, or safety requirements?
  • Who holds keys / access codes? What is the parking situation?
  • Are there HOA, building management, or security notifications required?

Site Visit Requirements

Always scout the location in person if it is a first-time client or a complex property. Google Earth does not show you the HVAC noise, the neighbor's barking dog, or the fact that the "natural light" is blocked by a new construction crane.

  • Photograph every room from the angles you intend to shoot
  • Note sunrise/sunset direction and times (PhotoPills or Sun Surveyor)
  • Identify all noise sources: HVAC, highways, construction, water features
  • Check power outlet locations for lighting
  • Confirm parking and gear unloading access
  • Look for staging needs: furniture placement, clutter removal
  • Flag any drone restrictions (airspace, power lines, trees)

Scheduling Considerations

You have 4 hours of genuinely good light on most properties. Plan the shoot as if you have 3. Cloud cover, client delays, and traffic will eat the fourth.

  • Exteriors first. Light changes fastest outside. Interiors can be lit artificially.
  • Schedule 30 min buffer before and after the client-provided window.
  • Confirm tenant/occupant availability 24 hours before. If they cancel, the shoot is compromised.
  • Double-check weather at 6 PM the day before. Have a rain date policy in the contract.

You are the entire crew. One mistake means there is no one to catch it. Run the checklist every single time.

Backup Protocol — Non-Negotiable

Follow this exactly. No shortcuts. No "I'll do it when I get home."

  1. Dual record if camera supports it. The FX30 has dual card slots. Use them.
  2. At every break: offload Card A to the Samsung T7 Shield (working drive).
  3. At lunch or mid-day: offload Card B to a WD My Passport (backup drive #1).
  4. Never travel with all media in one bag. Working drive goes in your backpack. Backup drive goes in a separate pocket or bag.
  5. Do not format cards until you have verified 3 copies exist: original card, working drive, backup drive.
  6. Keep client project files for 1 year minimum. After delivery, archive to both backup drives before clearing the working drive.
⚠ Reality Check
A single failed card on a Signature project means 6-8 hours of reshoots, a blown schedule, and potentially a lost client. The 15 minutes you spend backing up on-site saves you 15 hours of damage control.

Revision Policy

Your contract states 2 rounds of revisions included. Anything beyond that is billed at $150/hour or a flat $300 per additional round. State this upfront in the proposal. Clients respect boundaries when they are set before money changes hands.

Revision Round Definitions

Round 1 — Structural: Reorder scenes, swap music, trim or extend sections, replace shots from b-roll reserves.

Round 2 — Polish: Text changes, color tweak, audio level adjustments, end card updates.

Round 3+ — Billed: New shots not in original scope, complete music change, new graphics, change in runtime target. This is a change order, not a revision.

How to Handle "Can You Do It Cheaper?"

Never reply with a number. Reply with a scope reduction. If they want a lower price, they must accept fewer deliverables.

Script: "Can You Do It Cheaper?"

I can adjust the scope to fit your budget. Here are the options:

Option A: Reduce runtime from 3 min to 90 sec. Price: $3,800.
Option B: Remove drone and social cutdowns. Price: $4,200.
Option C: Deliver raw footage only, no editing. Price: $2,800.

The full Signature package at $5,500 remains the best value for what you described. Which direction works for you?

Upsell Scripts

Upsell: Drone + Same-Day Social Cuts

For $800 more, we can add drone coverage and deliver three 15-second social cuts the same day as the main video. Developers who use same-day cuts on LinkedIn see 3-4x engagement versus posting a week later. Want me to add that?

Upsell: Stills Package

I can pull 20 high-res still frames from the video and color-correct them for your listing platform. It is a $600 add-on that saves you hiring a separate photographer. Interested?

⚠ Never Discount Below These Floors

Essential floor: $1,800 (below this you lose money after true cost)
Signature floor: $4,200 (below this you are subsidizing the client's marketing)
Enterprise floor: $7,500 (below this the project complexity exceeds the reward)

If the prospect cannot meet the floor, refer them elsewhere. Protect your pricing integrity.

Section 06

Editor Management

Your time is worth $75-150/hr. Editing costs $25-40/hr. Do the math.

ℹ Brutal Truth
You cannot scale past $150K/year if you are still in Premiere Pro (or DaVinci) for 30 hours a week. Shooting and selling are the highest-value activities. Editing is a task to be delegated, not a craft to be hoarded.

Why Outsource

  • One solo shooter-editor can complete 30-40 projects per year maximum.
  • An editor working in parallel doubles your output without doubling your shoot days.
  • Your closing rate goes up when you are not exhausted from all-night edit sessions.
  • Editors who specialize in real estate cut faster and better than you do.

Platforms

Local Film Students Budget work, Essential tier overflow Cheap ($15-25/hr) but unreliable. Good for non-deadline filler.

Pay per project, not per hour. Hourly editing incentivizes slowness. Per-project rates align the editor's incentive with your deadline.

Quality Control: Editor Onboarding Kit

Every editor who works for Atlas Visual gets this kit before their first project. No exceptions. You cannot expect consistency without standards.

  • Brand guidelines PDF (fonts: Inter + Space Grotesk, color palette, logo files)
  • LUT pack (.cube files for Resolve/Premiere — your standard grade)
  • Music style guide (5 temp tracks showing vibe, tempo range, genre boundaries)
  • Title card templates (.mogrt for Premiere or .drp titles for Resolve)
  • Example edits (3-5 reference videos marked "This is the standard")
  • Footage naming convention: PROPERTYNAME_YYYYMMDD_CAMA/B_CARD01
  • Delivery spec sheet (resolution, codecs, frame rate, max file size)
  • Revision protocol: how to receive notes, how to deliver updates, turnaround times

Building a Bench of 3 Editors

⚠ Brutal Truth
One will ghost. One will be slow. One will be great. Plan for all three. Never give your only editor a project with a hard deadline. If you have one editor and they get sick, you are the editor now — and you just blew your shoot schedule for the next week.
Editor Bench Structure

Editor A (Primary — 60% of work): Fast, reliable, knows your style inside out. Turnaround: 3-5 days. Rate: standard.

Editor B (Secondary — 30% of work): Different strengths (motion graphics, color, sound design). Handles overflow when Editor A is booked. Turnaround: 5-7 days.

Editor C (Emergency — 10% of work): Specialist or backup. Higher rate. Only used for rush jobs or when A and B are unavailable. You pay them to stay on the bench.

Payment & 1099 Rules

  • Pay net-15 for reliable editors. Net-30 is standard but slower relationships die.
  • Use Melio, Bill.com, or ACH transfer. Avoid PayPal fees eating 3% of your margin.
  • Collect W-9 before first payment. Issue 1099-NEC by January 31 for anyone paid $600+ in a year.
  • Do not treat editors like employees. No set hours, no mandatory meetings, no company email. That is W-2 territory and the IRS will fine you.

Insurance Requirements

  • General Liability: $1M per occurrence / $2M aggregate. Required by most commercial property managers.
  • Equipment Insurance (Inland Marine): Covers theft, damage, loss. Cost: ~$400-600/year for $15K coverage.

The 15% Rule

ℹ Gear Discipline
In Year 1, no single equipment purchase should exceed 15% of trailing 3-month revenue. If you made $22,500 in the last quarter, your gear budget cap is $3,375. Anything above that is gambling with survival cash.

Gear ROI Test

Before any purchase over $1,000, answer these three questions with numbers:

1. Will this enable charging 15%+ more on the next 6 projects?

Example: New cinema camera enables "4K 120fps slow-motion" as a billed add-on at $400/project. 6 × $400 = $2,400. Purchase price $5,500. Payback: 14 projects.

Verdict: Marginal — verify against actual client demand.

2. Will this reduce production time by 20%+ on every project?

Example: New computer renders 40% faster. Saves 3 hrs/project × 30 projects × $95/hr = $8,550/year. Purchase price $3,200. Payback: 4.5 months.

Verdict: Smart Buy.

3. Does a paying client specifically require this capability?

Example: Client requires RAW 6K delivery. Current camera does not. Client revenue = $12,000. Purchase justified by confirmed revenue.

Verdict: Smart Buy.

Section 08

When Things Go Wrong

You will face all of these in Year 1. Probably multiple times. The question is whether you have a plan or you panic.

⚠ Brutal Truth
The difference between a professional and an amateur is not that professionals avoid problems. It is that professionals have already written the email template for when the problem happens.

Client Ghosts After Proposal

This is the most common failure mode. The proposal was good. They seemed interested. Then silence. Here is the exact follow-up sequence:

Touch Timing Medium Message
1Day 3EmailFriendly check-in. Any questions?
2Day 7EmailOffer to adjust scope/budget.
3Day 14Email"Should I close the file?"
4Day 21Phone5-minute call. Voice breaks through noise.
5Day 30NoneMove on. They are not buying.

After Touch 5, archive the lead. Do not stew. Do not take it personally. 70% of proposals die in silence. Your job is to send enough proposals that the 30% who respond keep you busy.

Project Scope Creeps

⚠ Scope Creep Warning
Scope creep is how you work 60 hours for $2,000. A client who asks for "one small thing" three times has added 8 hours of work. At your effective rate, that is $760 in free labor. The first time they ask for something outside scope, send a change order. No exceptions. Not even for "nice" clients.

Editor Misses Deadline

Your editor missed the delivery date. The client is expecting the cut tomorrow. What do you do?

  1. Do not panic-email the client. You have 24 hours of buffer built into every delivery date. Use it.
  2. Contact Editor B or C immediately. Offer a rush fee ($150-300) to take over. Send them the project files and notes tonight.
  3. If no editor is available, you edit it yourself. Cancel your plans. This is why you build buffer into deadlines.
  4. After delivery, fire the ghosting editor. Do not give them second chances on deadline-dependent work.
  5. Update your contract language: "Missed deadline without 48-hour notice incurs a 20% fee reduction."

Equipment Fails On Shoot

Camera error. Gimbal motor dies. Lens won't focus. You are on location with a client watching.

  • Always carry a backup body if possible. If not, your phone shoots 4K. Use it as a last resort and be transparent with the client.
  • Rental options in Portland/Vancouver: Pro Photo Supply (Portland), Lensrentals.com (overnight shipping). Budget $80-150/day for emergency body or lens rental.
  • Insurance claim: If the failure is due to covered damage/theft, file immediately. Document everything with photos. Payouts take 2-4 weeks.
  • Client communication: "We had an equipment issue on set. I have sourced a replacement and we will reshoot [specific shots] at no additional cost on [date]." Do not blame the gear. Take responsibility.

Client Refuses to Pay

This will happen. B2B late payment rate is 47%. Some clients will push it to 60 or 90 days. A few will refuse entirely.

Section 09

Seasonality Planning

December will be dead. Plan for it. The question is not whether the slow season comes. The question is whether you have cash when it arrives.

⚠ Brutal Truth
60-70% of your revenue will come in Q2 and Q3. If you spend every dollar you earn in June, you will not make rent in January. Most solo videographers fail in their first winter because they treated peak-season revenue as permanent.

Slow Season Revenue Diversification

When real estate video slows, you need alternative revenue streams that use your existing gear and skills:

  • Corporate headshots: $200-400/session, 1-hour turnaround with basic retouching.
  • Architectural photography: Many of your RE clients also need stills. Bundle or upsell.
  • Post-production for other videographers: Edit overflow work from wedding or event shooters who are slammed in Q4.
  • Stock footage: Upload b-roll rejects to Artgrid/Storyblocks. Pennies per download, but passive.
  • Personal brand content: Build your own YouTube/LinkedIn presence. Long-term inbound play.

Client Concentration Risk

⚠ Danger Zone
No single client should represent more than 30% of your monthly revenue. If one client pays 50% or more of your income, you do not have a business. You have an employer who does not give you benefits. If they leave, you die. Keep hustling for diversification even when the big client is keeping you busy.

Cash Flow Death Spiral

You shoot in Week 1. Invoice 50% deposit before shoot. You deliver in Week 3. Invoice remaining 50% on Net-30; payment arrives in Week 7. Your editor needs payment in Week 4. You are floating editor costs for 3-4 weeks before the client pays. If the client pays late (47% do), you are floating 6-8 weeks. This is where video businesses die.

Guardrails:

  • Structure payments as 40% booking / 40% production day / 20% final delivery for projects over $5,000.
  • Maintain a $10,000 operating reserve at all times. This is not profit. It is oxygen.
  • Pay editors net-15 if possible, but negotiate net-30 when cash is tight. Do not float costs you cannot cover.

Equipment Addiction vs Revenue Generation

The video industry is uniquely vulnerable to gear fetishism. Cameras are reviewed like sports cars. Forums convince you that a new sensor will double your rates. It will not.

Year 1 equipment reserve: $3,000-6,000 depending on revenue scenario. This is a replacement reserve for failure, not an upgrade fund for desire. If your current camera can deliver 4K 10-bit, you do not need a new camera. You need a new client.

The $250K Ceiling

⚠ The Honest Answer Is...
Without systems, you cannot grow past solo. Den Lennie, who has coached 178+ production companies, calls this the $250K ceiling. One shooter can only film and edit so many projects. At $5,500 average, you need 3.8 projects per month to hit $250K. That is 46 projects a year — a brutal pace with zero vacation, zero sick days, and zero margin for error. Breaking the ceiling requires higher prices, a team, or retainers. Preferably all three.

Survival Runway

You have 5-6 months of survival with zero revenue because your gear is paid off. That is your edge. Most new videographers have 2-3 months because they are making equipment payments. Use that runway wisely. It is not permission to move slowly. It is buffer to find product-market fit before you starve.

✓ Equipment-Free Launch Advantage

Your competitive moat is that you own your gear outright. Margins beat new competitors by 15-20%. You can undercut and still profit, or charge market rate and bank the difference. Client selectivity is a luxury that comes from low overhead. Do not give it away by buying gear you do not need.
