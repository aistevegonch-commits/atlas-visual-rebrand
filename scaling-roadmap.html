Phase 0

Pre-Launch

You're here. Probably. This phase feels productive. It's mostly admin. The real work hasn't started.

Goal: Ready to sell. Not ready to deliver at scale.

Legal & Identity

  • LLC formation + EIN
  • Business checking account
  • Insurance (BOP + E&O, ~$1,200/year)
  • Client contract templates
  • Subcontractor agreements

Digital Presence

  • Website live
  • LinkedIn company page
  • Portfolio / demo reel
  • Vimeo portfolio channel
  • Email signature + templates

Systems & Tools

  • Frame.io for client review
  • Artlist / Epidemic Sound
  • CRM (HubSpot free)
  • Proposal & invoicing system
  • Accounting (QuickBooks SE)

Network Prep

  • List 50 real estate contacts
  • Identify 100 LinkedIn targets
  • Join NAIOP, ULI, local associations
  • Prepare outreach scripts
  • Schedule 5-10 intro coffees
Exit Criteria: LLC formed, insurance active, website live, portfolio ready, pricing set, contracts ready. You can legally operate and confidently pitch.
Spending 3 months on a logo is not pre-launch. It's procrastination. Most people fail here because they confuse being busy with being ready.
Phase 1

Launch

Your first 3 months will be mostly rejection. If you have 0 conversations by Week 6, your pitch is broken. Not your luck.

Goal: First paid project + 3 prospects in pipeline. Key metric: conversations started, not deals closed.

Month 1: Warm Network

  • Reach out to every contact you know
  • Target: 5+ intro conversations
  • Film 1-2 spec pieces if portfolio is thin
  • Budget: $500 (tools, mailers)

Month 2: Cold Outreach + Content

  • 20 cold emails/week minimum
  • 2 LinkedIn posts/week
  • Film 2-3 spec videos
  • Target: 3+ discovery calls, 1+ proposal
  • Budget: $800 (mailers, Premium, shoots)

Month 3: First Closes

  • Close 1-3 projects at "founding client" pricing (~30% below target)
  • Collect testimonials + case studies
  • Document what worked and what didn't
  • Target: $2K+ revenue, 2+ testimonials

Phase 2

Validation

This is where most people quit. You will question if this was the right decision. Everyone does.

Goal: Prove you can get consistent work. Validation doesn't mean "I made money once." It means "I can make money again."

Months 4-6: Pricing Correction

  • Raise to market rate (no more founding discounts)
  • Target: 1-2 projects/month by Month 6
  • Focus: Signature tier ($2,995)
  • Build editor bench if volume exceeds capacity

Months 7-9: Repeatability

  • Systematize outreach: same volume every week
  • First retainer conversation with repeat client
  • Target: 2-3 projects/month
  • Revenue target: $5K-8K/month

Months 10-12: Cash Flow Stability

  • Deposit + final payment rhythm established
  • 3-month expense buffer achieved (~$15K cash)
  • Portfolio now has 5+ real projects
  • Target: 2-3 projects/month

Phase 3

Optimization

Now you have work. Can you do it without working 70 hours/week? This phase is boring. That's the point. Boring means it's working.

Goal: Systems, processes, editor bench. Build a business, not a job.

Systems & Templates

  • Proposal templates standardized
  • Client onboarding automated
  • CRM fully utilized with pipelines
  • Project handoff docs for editors
  • Invoice + follow-up automations

Editor Bench

  • 2+ reliable contract editors tested
  • 50%+ of editing outsourced
  • Style guide + LUTs shared
  • Turnaround times agreed in writing
  • Quality review checklist

Margin Focus

  • Stop taking Essential-tier unless it leads upstream
  • Negotiate editor rates with volume
  • Add upsells: social cuts, stills, drone
  • Track actual hours per project
  • Fire bottom 20% of clients by headache

Phase 4

Scale

This is where you either hire or stay small forever. Hiring is a $50K+ decision. Treat it like one.

Goal: First W-2 hire. Most who try to scale fail here. Cash flow kills them.

Pre-Hire (Months 18-24)

  • 6-month trailing avg >$18K/mo
  • Cash reserve >$25K
  • All processes documented (SOPs)
  • Turning away work or missing deadlines
  • Owner at capacity on sales + delivery

First W-2 Hire

  • Hire editor or assistant ($48K base + ~$6K loaded)
  • Owner transitions to sales + creative direction
  • Set 90-day onboarding plan with KPIs
  • Target: $20K+/mo after 3 months

Post-Hire (Months 24-36)

  • W-2 handles 60%+ of post-production
  • Owner sells full-time
  • Add second contract editor for overflow
  • Implement project management tool
  • Target: $25K+/mo by Month 30

Phase 5

Expansion

You probably won't get here. That's fine. This requires you to become a manager, not a filmmaker. Most creatives hate this.

Goal: Multi-city, multiple crews, or specialized niche dominance. You have a business that runs without you for 2+ weeks.

Year 3: Second Market

  • Local market generating >$250K/year
  • Expand to Seattle or Portland metro
  • Hire second shooter / editor in new market
  • Target: $400K-600K annual revenue

Year 4: Multi-Market

  • Add additional regional hub
  • Hire producer / account manager
  • Target: $600K-900K annual revenue
  • Team: 5-7 FTE

Year 5: Niche Dominance or Agency

  • Target: $500K-1M annual revenue
  • Team: 5-10 employees
  • Either own a niche or own a region
  • Owner is CEO, not filmmaker

Decision Gates

Green means go. Yellow means pivot. Red means quit. Quitting is a valid choice. Don't glamorize suffering.

Month 3 Gate

Proceed

1+ project booked or 3+ serious conversations in progress. You have proof the market responds.

Pivot

0 conversations but good feedback on pitch when you do get responses. Change niche, pricing, or channel.

Quit

0 conversations AND dread doing outreach. If you're not talking to prospects, you're not in business. You're hobbying.

The honest timeline: Most people think they'll have their first client in 30 days. Reality: 60-90 days is normal if you're doing the work. 120+ days with zero revenue means something is fundamentally wrong.

Month 6 Gate

Proceed

2+ clients, $5K+ total revenue, still have $4K+ cash. You're not thriving, but you're learning.

Pivot

Some interest but wrong niche or pricing. People want video, just not what you're offering at that price.

Quit

$0 revenue, $0 pipeline, miserable. 6 months with no revenue is not "building." It's waiting.

The honest timeline: Most people think they'll be at $10K/mo by Month 6. Reality: $2K-5K total revenue in the first 6 months is more typical for someone without an existing network.

Month 12 Gate

Proceed

$8K+/mo trailing, 3+ repeat clients, enjoying the work enough to keep going. Year 2 is warranted.

Pivot

Revenue there but unsustainable hours or bad clients. Fix the model before scaling.

Quit

Can't break $3K/mo, no repeat clients, burned out. Year 1 is the filter. Most don't make it to Year 2.

The honest timeline: Most people think $100K in Year 1 is achievable. Reality: $30K-80K is realistic. $100K+ requires existing relationships, prior reputation, or luck.

Month 18 Gate (Hire Decision)

Hire

$15K+/mo trailing, turning away work, documented processes. You have a business that needs people.

Delay

$12K+/mo but processes are a mess. Hiring with messy processes = paying someone to inherit your chaos.

Stay Solo

<$12K/mo trailing. Do not hire. Fix revenue first. A $50K salary obligation on $8K/mo revenue is how businesses die.

The honest timeline: Most people think they'll hire their first employee in Month 12. Reality: Month 18-24 is more realistic, and only if revenue is consistent. Most never hire.

Year 2 Gate

Expand / Scale

$20K+/mo, team of 2+, systems running. You have a real business. Keep building.

Stay Small

$15K/mo, happy, no desire to manage. Staying small is a valid choice. Don't let ambition guilt you into misery.

Shut Down / Sell

Revenue declining, burned out, no path forward. There is no shame in closing a business that isn't working.

The honest timeline: Most people think scaling is the only success path. Reality: a $15K/mo solo business with low overhead and no employees is an excellent outcome. Don't let Instagram entrepreneurs convince you otherwise.

Common Failure Patterns by Phase

You will experience 2-3 of these. The question is whether you recognize and fix them.

Phase 0: Perfectionism

Spending 3 months on a logo, website revisions, and business card design instead of selling. The best logo in the world won't pay your rent. A mediocre logo and 50 conversations will.

Phase 1: Fear of Outreach

Hiding behind "building the brand" because cold outreach feels bad. Sending 5 emails and quitting. Reality: you need 20+ per week for months. Volume is the only variable you control early on.

Phase 2: Underpricing

Charging $800 for a $5K project because you're "new." Every discount past your first 3 projects teaches the market you're cheap. Clients who pay $800 expect $800 work and complain anyway.

Phase 3: Working IN the Business

No systems, just hustle. Editing every project yourself because "no one can do it like me." You become a $75/hr editor instead of a $200/hr salesperson. The business owns you.

Phase 4: Hiring Too Fast

Bringing on a $4K/mo editor at $8K/mo revenue because you're exhausted. Exhaustion is not a hiring strategy. A W-2 employee is a $50K+/year obligation. Cash flow swings will kill you.

Phase 5: Founder Ego

Refusing to delegate the creative work because your identity is "filmmaker." The business stalls because everything requires you. If you still want to be hands-on in Phase 4, you don't want to scale. You want a high-paying job.

⚠

Pattern Recognition

Read this list monthly. When you feel resistance, ask: "Which failure pattern am I living right now?" The answer is usually obvious. The fix is usually uncomfortable.

Owner's Evolution

Your job description changes at every phase. If you still want to be hands-on in Phase 4, you don't want to scale. You want a high-paying job. That's fine. Be honest about it.

Phase 0-1
Salesperson Who Also Films
60% selling, 30% filming, 10% admin. You are not a creative. You are a founder with a camera. The camera is a tool, not an identity.
Phase 2
Filmmaker Who Also Sells
40% filming, 40% selling, 20% delivery. You have some work. You still do too much yourself. The transition begins here.
Phase 3
Business Owner Who Sometimes Films
20% filming, 60% selling + systems, 20% management. You start letting go of the work. This feels wrong. Do it anyway.
Phase 4
Manager Who Reviews Work
5% filming, 30% selling, 65% managing people and processes. If this sounds terrible to you, stop at Phase 3. There's no shame in it.
Phase 5
CEO Who Hires Managers
0% filming, 10% selling key accounts, 90% strategy + leadership. You run a business. You do not make videos. Most filmmakers never make this jump.

The Honest Question

Do you want to be a filmmaker with a good income, or a business owner who used to be a filmmaker? There is no right answer. There is only the answer you actually want, not the one that sounds impressive.

Reality Check

Year 1: $30K-80K is realistic. $100K+ requires existing relationships or luck. Year 2: $200K-400K is achievable only if Year 1 was solid. The jump from $80K to $200K is harder than the jump from $0 to $80K.

ⓘ

Break-Even Math

Your true break-even is 1.05 Signature projects/month ($2,995). Survival runway: 5-6 months with zero revenue on ~$10K capital. If you're not at break-even by Month 5, your pricing or volume is wrong.

The Brutal Truth Summary

Read this when you're feeling optimistic. Read it again when you're feeling defeated.

You have a 5-year plan. The market has other plans. Most successful videographers didn't follow a roadmap. They adapted constantly. This document is a compass, not a contract. Check it monthly. Adjust quarterly.

If you're not uncomfortable, you're not growing. If you're miserable, you're not sustainable. There is a difference between productive discomfort and destructive burnout. Learn it fast.

The goal isn't to hit every milestone. It's to still be in business in Year 3. Most video production companies don't make it 18 months. Surviving is winning.

Your network matters more than your hustle. If you know 5 people who can hire you, you'll beat someone with better gear and no connections. Hustle fills gaps. It doesn't replace relationships.

Quitting is a valid choice. If you hate selling, hate managing, and only want to film, there is nothing wrong with being a well-paid freelancer or taking a staff job. The "entrepreneur" badge is not worth your sanity.

This document was written for you, not for clients. Don't show it to prospects. Don't post it on LinkedIn. It's a private reference. If you start using it as marketing material, you've misunderstood its purpose.

Most of this won't happen exactly as planned. Plan for it anyway. The value is in the thinking, not the predictions.
